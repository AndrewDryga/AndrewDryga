# Boring Tech, High Leverage

Draft scaffold — outline subject to change.

## Premise

- “Boring” isn’t about stagnation—it’s about predictable, low‑variance building blocks that compound.
- Proven, well‑understood tools reduce failure modes, improve hiring/onboarding, and lower TCO.
- Keep the cognitive/operational budget for where it matters: the product and your differentiators.

## What “boring” means (and what it doesn’t)

- Boring = widely adopted, well‑documented, strong defaults, clear SLO stories, known runbooks.
- Not boring = cutting‑edge for its own sake, bespoke/configuration‑heavy stacks, one‑person expertise.
- Boring ≠ never innovate. It means you choose innovation wedges intentionally and isolate the blast radius.

## Operating principles

- Default to boring for:
  - Data storage, message queues, CI/CD, runtime, auth, metrics/logging/tracing.
- Innovate where:
  - It’s truly your differentiator or gives 10x leverage (UX, domain logic, ML signal, workflow design).
- Tight interfaces:
  - Wrap infra behind stable contracts; swap internals with minimal surface impact.
- SLOs and budgets first:
  - Every “non‑boring” component must carry an SLO + owner + rollback plan + cost guardrails.

## Decision rubric (5 questions)

1) Is there a simpler, more boring option that is “good enough” and available now?
2) What is the reliability story? (SLO, rollback, on‑call burden, known failure modes)
3) What is the total cost of ownership over 12–24 months (licensing + infra + people)?
4) What is the replaceability plan? (Abstraction, migration path, timebox)
5) What evidence do we have that this increases throughput or reduces risk vs. alternatives?

## Patterns that pay rent

- Paved roads:
  - Templates, scaffolds, and golden paths that encode boring defaults (lint, test, deploy, observe).
- Small wedges:
  - Pilot new tech in a non‑critical slice with explicit exit criteria; measure outcomes.
- Thin adapters:
  - Hide vendor specifics behind internal modules; track version and drift centrally.
- Sane limits:
  - Bound concurrency, queues, retries; add backpressure and circuit breakers by default.

## Anti‑patterns

- “Future proofing” your way into complexity—premature abstraction and multi‑cloud for no reason.
- Tool sprawl: five databases, three queues, two front‑ends because “it’s interesting.”
- Lone‑expert risk: one engineer understands it; everyone else fears touching prod.
- SLO theater: dashboards nobody uses; incidents handled by folklore instead of runbooks.

## Adoption playbook (lightweight)

- Start with an inventory:
  - Critical path, dependencies, owners, SLOs, cost hotspots.
- Consolidate:
  - Reduce duplicate tools; pick one boring, proven choice per category.
- Pave:
  - Turn the consolidated choice into starter kits and CI templates; enforce via checks, not memos.
- Guard and measure:
  - Error budgets, burn‑rate alerts, cost dashboards with deltas; weekly 30‑min review.

## Hiring and DX benefits

- Faster onboarding: standard tools, familiar defaults, fewer “mysterious” edges.
- Better interviews: measure thinking and judgment over specific niche tech.
- Easier rotation/on‑call: runbooks match reality; incidents de‑risked by predictable behavior.

## Checklist

- [ ] We have a boring, paved path for: build, deploy, metrics, logs, traces, auth, data, queue.
- [ ] Every non‑boring component has: SLO, owner, rollback plan, cost guardrails.
- [ ] One‑page decision memo exists for new tech: hypothesis, alternatives, exit criteria.
- [ ] We measure throughput and reliability before/after adoption.
- [ ] Standard adapters wrap vendor specifics; replaceability is tested in a sandbox.

## Notes

- Boring tech is a speed strategy: fewer surprises, lower variance, better runtime economics.
- Save novelty for where it wins the market—make the rest invisible and uneventful.
