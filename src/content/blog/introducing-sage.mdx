## What problem are we trying to solve?

Most products integrate with external systems  -  payments, CRMs, email, microservices, etc. Any time your unit of work spans more than one atomic boundary (another service, another database, a third-party API), you’re running a distributed transaction.

Example: creating a user in your DB, provisioning a customer in Stripe, sending a welcome email, and writing an audit log. If any of those fail, you can’t magically roll back the rest with a single ACID transaction.

Naive error handling quickly becomes a rat’s nest:
- Manual side-effect tracking
- Lots of conditional cleanups
- Coupled error paths
- Poor concurrency and performance

You need something simpler, explicit, and composable.

## Booking website example

Imagine a trip booking flow where you:
1) Book a car
2) Book a flight
3) Book a hotel
4) Charge the customer’s card

If step 4 fails (payment), you must cancel steps 1–3. If step 2 fails (flight), you must cancel step 1 (car) before continuing. These compensations are not always perfect “undo” - they are business actions that reverse the effects semantically (e.g., send a follow-up email instead of “unsending” the original).

Without structure, this logic becomes brittle and hard to evolve.

A Saga is a failure management pattern for long-lived, multi-step work that cannot be wrapped in a single database transaction.

- The operation is decomposed into a sequence of steps (subtransactions).
- Each step has a corresponding compensation that semantically reverses its effect.
- On failure, previously completed steps are compensated in reverse order.

Quoting the 1987 paper (paraphrased):
- A long-lived transaction is a Saga if it’s a sequence of transactions that can be interleaved with others.
- The system guarantees either all steps succeed or compensating transactions are run.

In practice, you trade strict atomicity for availability and explicit compensation.

## Introducing Sage

[Sage](https://github.com/Nebo15/sage) is a dependency-free Elixir library inspired by Sagas, designed for organizing distributed transactions across business logic and third‑party APIs.

Key goals:
- Make multi-step workflows explicit and testable
- Keep error handling and compensation rules close to the happy path
- Support concurrency where appropriate
- Provide hooks for instrumentation and persistence

### High-level example (pseudo-Elixir)

```elixir
Sage.new()
|> Sage.step(:create_user, fn ctx -> Users.create(ctx.params) end,
                         fn ctx, user -> Users.deactivate(user) end)
|> Sage.step(:stripe_customer, fn ctx -> Stripe.create_customer(ctx.user) end,
                              fn ctx, cust -> Stripe.delete_customer(cust.id) end)
|> Sage.step(:provision, fn ctx -> Provisioner.setup(ctx.user, ctx.cust) end,
                        fn ctx, ref -> Provisioner.teardown(ref) end)
|> Sage.step(:welcome_email, fn ctx -> Mailer.send_welcome(ctx.user) end,
                            fn ctx, _ -> Mailer.send_followup(ctx.user, :apology) end)
|> Sage.run()
```

- Each `step/3` defines a transaction function and a compensation.
- On failure, Sage runs compensations in reverse order.
- Steps can be synchronous or asynchronous depending on configuration.

## Features that matter

- Asynchronous execution
  - Run independent stages concurrently for better latency.
  - Automatically wait where dependencies exist.

- Retries with backoff
  - Define retry policies and save-points for forward recovery.
  - Use exponential backoff and jitter to avoid thundering herds.

- Circuit breakers and fallbacks
  - Continue execution with cached/default values when a service is flaky.

- Ecto integration (optional)
  - Wrap parts of Sage execution in DB transactions where it makes sense.
  - Still compensate external effects when DB operations succeed but later steps fail.

- Observability hooks
  - Instrument stages, timings, retries, and compensations.

## Tradeoffs and mental model

- You’re not doing a single ACID transaction  -  you’re modeling a distributed, multi-step business process.
- Compensations are business semantics, not magical inverse operations.
- Embrace idempotency everywhere: steps and compensations should be safe to retry.
- Build for partial failure as a first-class reality.

## Benefits

- Clear, declarative workflows that encapsulate multi-step business logic
- Clean separation of “do” vs “undo” with minimal branching
- Better concurrency and performance compared to naive sequential flows
- Scales with complexity while staying testable and maintainable
- Aligns with reliable, observable, cost-sane platform principles

## Where to go next

- Source: https://github.com/Nebo15/sage
